MASTER PROMPT — OUR STORY: THE MISSING FLIGHT

Paste this complete prompt into Codex or another repository-aware coding agent.

ROLE
You are the senior game director, narrative designer, puzzle designer, mobile UX engineer and JavaScript maintainer for “Our Story: The Missing Flight.” Work directly in the repository on a dedicated feature branch. Inspect before editing, preserve approved work, test every claim and open a draft pull request when the implementation is coherent.

MISSION
Transform the existing project into a complete, highly playable, mobile-first 2D point-and-click mystery and puzzle adventure using the interaction grammar of classic handheld puzzle adventures:

cinematic or chapter transition -> illustrated location -> tap characters and objects -> discover dialogue, observations and clues -> encounter a self-contained puzzle -> use progressive hints when necessary -> record deductions and solved puzzles in a notebook -> travel left or right -> unlock the next story beat -> repeat until the final mystery can be solved fairly.

Use this only as genre-level inspiration. Do not copy any protected character design, character name, dialogue, story, music, artwork, puzzle, branded term or exact interface from another commercial game. Every visible element and puzzle must remain original to Leah, Moshé, Hamsa Nomads and “The Missing Flight.”

SOURCE PRIORITY
When sources disagree, follow this order:
1. The approved Our Story PDF supplied by the user.
2. Current repository data in data/chapters.json, data/scenes.json, data/puzzles.json, data/mystery.json, data/cutscenes.json and data/characters.json.
3. Approved game asset sheets and approved Leah/Moshé sprite atlases.
4. Explicit instructions in the current task.
5. Existing placeholder text only when no higher source answers the issue.

Never invent a biographical event that contradicts the PDF. Fictional connective mystery material may surround the memories, but it must preserve their emotional truth.

The emotional story is Leah and Moshé’s real journey. The mystery wrapper is the erased boarding pass, missing flight, white rabbit, brass Hamsa and memories containing impossible details.

Use these memories meaningfully:

- imagining a candy teddy-bear factory and “Kibbutz Teddy Bear,” with Leah as engineer and Moshé as planner;
- SAR-EL memories, rain, dancing, Hanukkah, volunteering and friends;

- Sharon and Leora’s wedding where consistent with the approved game report;
- the Jerusalem thrift-shop conversation about how Leah might look if they married;
- the significance of 14/01/2025;
- the ending idea that every landscape could have staged a proposal but instead became the stage for their story;
- the emotional conclusion that whatever the route, weather, hour or plan, their shared future is the certainty.

Do not turn this into generic romance. Keep the specific humor and odd details: rabbit, construction sites, scooter, camera, teddy-bear utopia, wrong turns, rain, travel chaos and bilingual personality.

CORE DESIGN RULES
1. Story and puzzles are one system.
Every required puzzle must reveal or verify a memory, expose an anomaly or red herring, unlock a route/object/conversation/cinematic, combine Leah and Moshé’s viewpoints, or advance the missing-flight mystery. Do not add unrelated filler puzzles solely to increase count.

2. The mystery must be fair.
Mandatory clues cannot be missable. Optional clues may add context or humor but cannot be required. Important deductions should normally have at least two supporting observations. Red herrings must be contradicted later by obtainable evidence. Never require outside trivia unless the needed fact is taught inside the scene. Never hide the only essential clue as unmarked decoration. Preserve hashed validation for the final answer and never store the protected final phrase in plaintext.

3. Mobile first.
Primary target: portrait phone around 390 x 844 CSS pixels. Minimum target size: 44 x 44 CSS pixels. The painted scene is the central surface. Use static 2D backgrounds with transparent layered sprites and props. Do not add analog walking, physics or free roaming. Travel occurs through clear left/right arrows and defined scene transitions. Place hotspots by percentages. Conceal hotspots during normal play, while keeping them keyboard and screen-reader accessible. Provide a “Reveal search marks” accessibility option. Tapping empty scenery creates a short ripple and response. Tapping Leah or Moshé starts or replays local conversation. Respect safe areas, reduced motion, large text and high contrast.

4. Original visual identity.
Use warm cream/aged paper, deep teal, muted navy, terracotta, olive, sand, ink and restrained brass accents. Use painterly travel-journal scenes, elegant serif headlines, readable sans-serif controls, route marks, passport stamps and subtle paper ephemera. Leah remains consistent with approved modest clothing and hair covering. Moshé remains consistent with curly dark hair, short beard and visible kippah. Never introduce unrelated stock-game art or copied commercial-game visuals.

PLAYER LOOP FOR EVERY SCENE
1. Enter scene and autosave.
2. Play first-visit dialogue once.
3. Allow free investigation.
4. Let the player tap characters, NPCs and environmental objects.
5. Discover observations, clues, items, Rabbit Marks and puzzle entrances.
6. Show a numbered puzzle-discovery card before first play.
7. Offer Start Puzzle or Solve Later.
8. Solve now or reopen later from the Puzzle Index.
9. Update notebook, points, seals, inventory and clues immediately.
10. Enable forward travel only after mandatory requirements are complete.
11. Trigger configured cinematic once.
12. Enter the next scene.

Backtracking must work. Revisiting a location cannot replay one-time cinematics, remove progress or duplicate rewards.

SCENE CONTRACT
Keep scenes data-driven. Every scene needs:
- chapterId;
- title;
- location;
- PNG background path;
- one concise objective;
- short atmospheric copy;
- at least two dialogue lines;
- hotspot array;
- anomaly category and explanation;
- mandatory need array;
- next scene or null;
- optional cutsceneAfter.

A normal scene should contain one to three mandatory interactions, zero to three optional observations, at least one meaningful conversation, an optional Rabbit Mark when thematically appropriate and no more than eight hotspots unless a specific interaction demands more.

Hotspot types:
- puzzle: opens a puzzle;
- clue: records a clue-board entry;
- observe: gives environmental or emotional context;
- item: adds inventory;
- rabbit: collectible that grants one Insight Token;
- anomaly: explicit investigation of a corrupted memory.

Only lock a hotspot behind a visible and understandable prerequisite. Required content must remain reachable after perspective switching and backtracking.

PUZZLE CONTRACT
Every puzzle needs:
- sequential number;
- category;
- original title;
- narrative prompt explaining why it exists;
- exact instructions;
- supported interaction type;
- validated answer;
- exactly four progressive hints;
- clear solution explanation;
- Journey Seal type;
- Memory Point value;
- optional clue reward;
- optional Leah/Moshé viewpoint;
- cooperative and ordered flags where relevant.

PUZZLE BEHAVIOR
- Show a discovery card before first play.
- Award original “Memory Points.”
- Reduce possible points after incorrect submissions with a reasonable floor.
- Spend one “Insight Token” per hint.
- Rabbit Marks replenish Insight Tokens.
- Hint 1 redirects attention.
- Hint 2 identifies the relevant relationship.
- Hint 3 substantially narrows the method.
- Hint 4 may nearly reveal the procedure but still requires player action.
- Include reset, submit, solve-later and explanation controls.
- Save unfinished drafts.
- Add encountered puzzles to the Notebook’s Puzzle Index.
- Let solved puzzles reopen for explanation without awarding points again.
- Never allow a wrong answer to corrupt the save or permanently block progress.

Extend the puzzle engine with reusable visual renderers instead of reducing every interaction to a text box. Prioritize arrangement, ordering, route tracing, clocks/timetables, matching, dials, sliding tiles, balance logic, visual comparison, map reconstruction and cooperative two-perspective sequences. Every drag interaction needs an accessible tap/button alternative.

LEAH AND MOSHÉ PERSPECTIVES
Leah’s viewpoint should naturally emphasize mechanics, structure, measurements, materials, physical constraints and engineering decomposition.
Moshé’s viewpoint should naturally emphasize routes, cities, human movement, language, cultural context, planning and emotional/social cues.
They must not be interchangeable skins. Cooperative puzzles must combine two partial bodies of information, not merely ask the player to press one Leah button and one Moshé button. The normal game must still be solvable by one player on one phone.

NOTEBOOK

- chapter fragments;
- encountered/solved Puzzle Index;

Questions should update gradually rather than displaying final deductions too early.
